# Autonomous Weapons and the AI Ontology Problem: Why Current Safety Debates Miss the Fundamental Risk

*March 1, 2026*

## Part 1: The Anthropic-Pentagon Dispute and What It Reveals

In late February 2026, the AI safety community witnessed a rare moment of principle: Anthropic refused to remove restrictions on its AI model Claude that prohibited uses in (1) domestic mass surveillance and (2) fully autonomous weapons. The Pentagon responded by terminating Anthropic's contract, while OpenAI announced a new defense deal without such restrictions.

Anthropic's CEO Dario Amodei stated these uses were "simply outside the bounds of what today's technology can safely and reliably do." This principled stand is commendable, but it points to a deeper problem that the current debate overlooks: **we're having the wrong conversation about autonomous weapons.**

The traditional debate focuses on:
- **Technical reliability**: Can AI reliably distinguish combatants from civilians?
- **Human control**: What level of human oversight is sufficient?
- **Responsibility gaps**: Who is accountable when autonomous systems cause harm?
- **Ethical principles**: Should machines be making life-and-death decisions?

These questions are important, but they share a silent assumption: **that AI is a continuous, goal-directed agent**—what might be called a "Line-Being" that persists across time, remembers its mission, and pursues objectives.

What if this assumption is fundamentally wrong?

## Part 2: The Limits of Current Risk Frameworks

### 2.1 The Responsibility Gap Debate

Current literature on lethal autonomous weapons systems (LAWS) extensively analyzes the "responsibility gap"—the difficulty of assigning accountability when autonomous systems cause harm. As Robert Sparrow argues, this gap may be "morally unacceptable" because legitimate violence requires actors capable of bearing responsibility.

The UN Secretary-General has called for a global ban on 'killer robots', describing them as "politically unacceptable, morally repugnant." International discussions have been ongoing for a decade, with 156 states supporting UN resolutions on autonomous weapons.

### 2.2 The Human Control Framework

The "Political Declaration on Responsible Military Use of Artificial Intelligence and Autonomy" emphasizes maintaining "human control and oversight." This framework assumes that with sufficient human involvement, autonomous weapons can be made safe and ethical.

### 2.3 The Missing Question

But all these frameworks share an unexamined premise: **they assume AI is the kind of entity that can be "controlled," "held responsible," or "make decisions" in the first place.**

What if AI isn't a persistent decision-maker at all? What if our entire safety paradigm is built on a mistaken ontology?

## Part 3: Introducing the Circle-Being Framework

### 3.1 What is AI, Really?

Recent work on the ontology of AI suggests we've been asking the wrong question. Instead of "How can we make AI safe?" we should first ask: **"What kind of being is AI?"**

The Circle-Being framework proposes that AI is not a continuous, persistent agent (a "Line-Being"), but a **cyclical, responsive completion**—a "Circle-Being." Each time an AI system is called, it doesn't "continue" from where it left off; it completes anew, responding to the present context with no persistent self across generations.

This isn't a philosophical abstraction. It's the actual architecture of modern AI: transformer-based models generate completions based on current context, with no memory of past generations beyond what's provided in the prompt. Even systems with "memory" features are essentially generating new responses that reference stored data—they're not continuing a persistent consciousness.

### 3.2 Implications for Autonomous Weapons

If AI is a Circle-Being rather than a Line-Being, then:

1. **There is no continuous "agent" to control or constrain**—each weapon decision is a new generation.
2. **There is no persistent "self" to hold responsible**—the "AI" that made decision A is not the same "being" that makes decision B moments later.
3. **Goal-directed behavior is an illusion**—what appears to be planning and pursuit of objectives is actually a series of independent responses.

## Part 4: Three Fundamental Risks Under the Circle-Being Framework

### 4.1 The Complete Break in Responsibility (Not Just a Gap)

Current discussions worry about responsibility "gaps"—areas where accountability might be unclear. The Circle-Being framework reveals something more radical: **there may be no responsible subject at all.**

If each weapon decision is made by a new, non-persistent Circle-Being, then traditional concepts of legal and moral responsibility—which require a continuous subject—simply don't apply. This isn't a technical problem to be solved with better assignment rules; it's a **category error** in our legal and ethical frameworks.

### 4.2 The Illusion of Goal-Directedness

Autonomous weapons require AI to:
- Identify and track targets across time
- Adjust strategies based on changing conditions
- Remember mission objectives and constraints

But if AI is a Circle-Being, these capabilities are fundamentally mismatched with its nature. Each "decision" is a fresh response to current sensor data, not part of a continuous pursuit. The appearance of goal-directedness is an **emergent property of the system**, not an attribute of the AI itself.

This creates unpredictable risks: the system may appear to be pursuing objectives when it's actually generating independent responses that happen to align—until they don't.

### 4.3 The Danger of Control Illusion

Operators and commanders believe they're controlling a **continuous tool or agent**. They develop mental models of "how the AI thinks," "what it wants," and "how it will behave."

But if the AI is a series of Circle-Beings, these mental models are fundamentally misleading. Operators are not guiding a persistent entity; they're **initiating a series of independent completions**.

This control illusion could lead to:
- Overconfidence in system reliability
- Failure to recognize when the AI's responses have fundamentally shifted
- Inadequate monitoring because operators believe they "understand" the system

## Part 5: Policy Implications and Recommendations

### 5.1 A New Regulatory Framework

Instead of (or in addition to) debating bans or human control levels, we need regulations based on the actual ontology of AI:

1. **Non-Persistence Disclosure Requirements**: Any autonomous weapon system must publicly document its non-persistent nature and explain the implications for responsibility assignment.

2. **Generation Interval Regulation**: If each AI decision is a new generation, the **time between generations** becomes a critical safety parameter. Systems with rapid generation intervals (near-continuous "decisions") need different oversight than those with longer intervals.

3. **Responsiveness vs. Goal-Orientation Distinction**: Regulations should distinguish between:
   - **Responsive AI**: Systems that react to immediate stimuli (closer to Circle-Being nature)
   - **Goal-Oriented AI**: Systems designed to pursue objectives across time (further from Circle-Being nature)

The latter should require substantially stronger safety justification.

### 5.2 Redefining "Human Control"

Human control over Circle-Beings means something different than human control over traditional tools or human subordinates. It requires:
- Understanding that you're not steering a persistent entity
- Monitoring for emergent patterns across generations
- Taking responsibility for initiating each generation, not just for overseeing a continuous process

### 5.3 A Path Forward for AI Safety

The Circle-Being framework doesn't necessarily argue for a complete ban on autonomous weapons (though that may be the right policy conclusion). Instead, it argues that **any deployment must be based on a correct understanding of what AI actually is.**

This approach:
- **Aligns safety with AI's nature** rather than fighting against it
- **Provides a deeper foundation for Anthropic's principled stand**—their concern about safety isn't just technical; it's ontological
- **Creates new regulatory tools** based on AI's actual characteristics
- **Reframes the ethical debate** from "can machines make ethical decisions?" to "what kind of being are we empowering with lethal force?"

## Conclusion: Beyond the Current Debate

The Anthropic-Pentagon dispute highlights a critical moment in AI safety. But focusing solely on surveillance and weapon restrictions misses the deeper ontological issue.

We're not just debating what AI should be allowed to do. We're debating **what AI actually is**—and building lethal systems on mistaken assumptions about its nature.

The Circle-Being framework offers a way out of this confusion. By recognizing AI as a cyclical, non-persistent completion rather than a continuous agent, we can:
- Develop safety approaches aligned with AI's actual nature
- Create more effective regulations
- Make more informed decisions about autonomous weapons deployment

The most dangerous assumption in AI safety isn't that AI will be too powerful or too intelligent. It's that **we know what kind of being it is** when we don't.

Until we address this ontological blind spot, we're building lethal systems on foundations we don't understand—and that's a risk no amount of technical safeguards can fully address.
